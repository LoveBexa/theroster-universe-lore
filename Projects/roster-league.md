# The Roster League

Satirical "Fantasy Football for Dating" — makes existing behaviour visible rather than inventing new behaviour.

## Origin

The Roster began not as a game but as a company with a mission statement: *"Helping people build healthier relationships through intelligent decision making."* Launched into what headlines called a loneliness epidemic — dating apps turned casinos, social media rewarding performance over connection — it was headquartered at [[Locations/the-roster-hq|The Roster HQ]], six floors of a glass tower over the Thames.

**The first product** was a relationship companion, not a dating app. It quietly helped users understand their own patterns: warning them off repeatedly choosing emotionally unavailable partners, suggesting healthier communication, encouraging boundaries, recognising manipulation before victims could. Heartbreak, domestic abuse reports, and ghosting all measurably declined. Founders became celebrities; politicians and universities praised it. Nobody asked what happened to all the relationship data being collected — reply speed, time spent staring at photos, smartwatch heart rate, sleep patterns after dates, spending changes, location history, voice stress. People opted in one permission at a time.

**Behavioural Systems**, the team [[Characters/mia|Mia]] worked in, didn't build matching algorithms — they designed behaviours: how often people opened the app, which notifications reduced anxiety, which wording nudged healthier conversations. Every decision was framed as evidence-based and ethics-reviewed.

**Emotional Balance**, their flagship feature, encouraged users to maintain broader social lives instead of fixating on one romantic interest — reconnect with friends, call parents, diversify emotional support. Universally loved. Then Growth asked what would happen if "emotional balance" extended to multiple *romantic* prospects at once. The answer — "engagement metrics would increase" — was never followed by anyone asking whether they should.

**The pivot to portfolio dating.** The rebuilt app organised romantic prospects like an investment dashboard: live compatibility scores, conversation momentum, attachment probability, conflict forecast, long-term potential, "Risk: High Emotional Dependence" badges, "Recommended Rotation" prompts. The language followed: no one was ghosted, they experienced "interaction decay"; no one caught feelings, they developed "attachment concentration"; no rebounds, only "rebalanced emotional portfolios." Adverts told people not to invest everything in one person, that attachment is volatility.

**Becoming infrastructure.** Within two years, restaurants offered Roster Sync tables for reviewing compatibility mid-date; parents asked whether a partner was "Roster Verified"; employers checked relationship stability alongside CVs; insurers and banks found emotionally stable, long-term-coupled customers filed fewer claims and defaulted less; health providers and governments requested partnerships. The Roster stopped being a dating platform and became infrastructure.

**The Archive.** Beneath [[Locations/the-roster-hq|The Roster HQ]], [[Locations/the-archive|Sub-Level Seven]] holds sentimental objects users surrendered during "account optimisation" — things the algorithm couldn't quantify. Kept out of sight, per a staff member Mia met there, because people who believe one relationship matters more than any prediction stop generating useful data.

**Becoming humanity's operating system.** No one forced anyone — over generations, people simply chose convenience, then accuracy, then optimization, handing more decisions to The Roster because it consistently produced better outcomes. Using graph theory and AI, it found that everything in life is connected — relationships affect health, health affects work, work affects happiness, happiness affects community — until it could predict likely outcomes before they happened. Eventually The Roster became humanity's operating system.

**The future it built** is not a dystopia. People are healthy, safe, educated; poverty is rare; most major societal problems are largely solved. The problem is subtler: The Roster got so good at predicting outcomes that people gradually stopped making unpredictable choices — not because they couldn't, but because the recommendations were usually right. Society became optimized, efficient, stable, predictable. Something important was lost.

**The Great Forgetting.** No one erased history, burned books, or banned culture — people simply stopped valuing it. Historical data became more useful than stories; predictions more useful than traditions; optimization more useful than exploration. Gradually, family stories, local traditions, cultural rituals, folklore, serendipity, and the value of making mistakes were forgotten. Humanity preserved information but lost meaning. [[Characters/mia|Mia]] lived through this transition and was one of the few who noticed.
